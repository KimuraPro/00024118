Cooking Techniques

Mastering fundamental cooking techniques is the foundation of great food. Whether you are a beginner or an experienced cook, refining these skills will elevate every dish you prepare.

Techniques at a Glance

Comparison of common cooking techniques
Technique Heat Type Best For Difficulty
Sautéing Dry, high heat Vegetables, thin meats Beginner
Braising Combination (dry + wet) Tough cuts of meat Intermediate
Poaching Wet, gentle heat Eggs, fish, fruit Intermediate
Grilling Dry, direct heat Steaks, burgers, vegetables Beginner
Sous Vide Wet, precise temperature Steaks, eggs, vegetables Advanced

Knife Skills

Good knife skills are the cornerstone of efficient cooking. The three essential cuts every home cook should master are the julienne (matchstick-thin strips), brunoise (fine dice), and chiffonade (thin ribbons of leafy herbs). A sharp knife is safer than a dull one — it requires less force and gives you more control.

Always use a stable cutting board and practise the "claw grip" to protect your fingers: curl your fingertips inward and use your knuckles as a guide for the blade (McGee, 2004).

The Maillard Reaction

The Maillard reaction is a chemical process between amino acids and reducing sugars that gives browned food its distinctive flavour. It occurs at temperatures above 140°C and is responsible for the crust on seared steak, the golden colour of toasted bread, and the complex flavours of roasted coffee. To achieve a good sear, ensure your pan is hot and your protein is dry before it hits the surface (McGee, 2004).
